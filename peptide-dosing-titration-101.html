What Is Titration?

Titration is the process of gradually increasing (or sometimes decreasing) your dose over time to find the optimal balance between effectiveness and tolerability. Proper titration is one of the most important skills in peptide research — it minimizes side effects while maximizing research outcomes.

Why Titration Matters
Starting at a full dose dramatically increases the risk of side effects. Gradual titration allows your system to adapt, reduces adverse reactions, and helps identify your personal optimal dose.

Core Titration Principles

Start Low, Go Slow
Begin at the lowest effective research dose. Increase only after confirming tolerance at the current level. Most protocols recommend 2–4 weeks between dose increases.
Listen to Your Body
Side effects are signals. Mild nausea or fatigue may be acceptable during titration, but severe symptoms warrant pausing or reducing the dose. Never push through significant adverse effects.
Document Everything
Keep a log of dose, timing, side effects, and research outcomes. This data is invaluable for adjusting your protocol and identifying patterns.

Growth Hormone Secretagogues (Ipamorelin, CJC-1295, Tesamorelin)
These peptides typically do not require formal titration. Start at the full research dose from day one:
Ipamorelin: 200–300 mcg 1–3x daily
CJC-1295: 100–200 mcg 1–2x daily
Tesamorelin: 1–2 mg daily

Monitoring During Titration

Track These Weekly:
• Body weight and measurements
• Energy levels and sleep quality
• Appetite and food intake
• Gastrointestinal symptoms
• Injection site reactions
• Mood and cognitive function

When to Pause or Reduce Dose

Pause or reduce if you experience:
• Severe or persistent nausea/vomiting
• Significant fatigue that impairs daily function
• Injection site reactions that worsen or spread
• Signs of allergic reaction (rash, swelling, difficulty breathing)
• Unexplained rapid heart rate or blood pressure changes
Important: Titration schedules are general guidelines. Individual responses vary significantly. Always prioritize safety over speed. Consult research protocols and adjust based on your specific research parameters.
